INNOVATION SHOWCASE — PRESENTATION OUTLINE & SPEAKER NOTES
"From Reports to Signals: Building a Culture of Continuous Improvement"
20 Minutes | All IT Employees | Neil Alcorn
================================================================================

TONE GUIDE (read before presenting)
-------------------------------------
Humble. We built something useful, not something perfect.
Helpful. Every metric exists to help the team, not evaluate it.
Process-first. We measure work, not workers.
Hopeful. This is a beginning, not a destination.

Keep it conversational. You are not teaching — you are sharing.
The dashboards are the proof. The story is the point.

================================================================================
SLIDE 1 — "WHERE DOES YOUR WORK COME FROM?"
Time: 2 minutes
================================================================================

HEADLINE:
  "You can't improve what you can't see."

VISUAL SUGGESTION:
  Simple flow diagram — a value stream in a Lean Office context:
    [Request Comes In] → [Work Gets Sized] → [Work Gets Done] → [Value Delivered]
  OR: pull your Lean Office value stream map slide from LSS training.
  Keep it generic — this applies to any IT role.

BULLET POINTS TO HIT:
  • Every one of us is part of a process — even if we don't call it that
  • Work comes from somewhere. It goes somewhere. There's a customer at the end.
  • Lean Office asks: where is value created? Where is it lost?
  • We spend most of our time focused on the task — rarely on the flow
  • This dashboard project started with one question: "What is our process actually doing?"

SPEAKER NOTES:
  Open with a question to the room — don't make it rhetorical, let it land.
  "Think about the last piece of work you completed. Where did it come from?
  Who asked for it? How long did it sit before you picked it up? How long did
  it take? Did it come back for rework?"

  Most people have never been asked to think about their work as a process.
  That's not a criticism — it's an opportunity.

  Loosely connect to the book: the foundation of Lean thinking is understanding
  your value stream before you try to improve it. You can't improve what you
  can't see. That's all this is.

  Transition: "We had a tool — Power BI. It told us a lot. But it told us
  about the past. We wanted something that could help us see the present —
  and maybe predict the future."

================================================================================
SLIDE 2 — "WE HAD REPORTS. WE NEEDED SIGNALS."
Time: 2 minutes
================================================================================

HEADLINE:
  "Reports tell you what happened. Signals tell you what to do."

VISUAL SUGGESTION:
  Two-column side by side:
    LEFT  — Power BI screenshot (a real one, something clean and familiar)
    RIGHT — ADO Dashboard screenshot (Dev dashboard or Leadership SPC chart)
  Label them simply: "Reporting Tool" | "Signal Tool"
  No winners or losers — just different jobs.

BULLET POINTS TO HIT:
  • Power BI is excellent — we still use it and will continue to
  • What it doesn't do well: real-time team visibility, ADO-native signal detection
  • We asked: what if the team could see their own flow without waiting for a report?
  • Zero new infrastructure — built on top of tools we already pay for
  • The team owns it. It isn't run by IT or BI. Anyone with a PAT can use it.

SPEAKER NOTES:
  Be generous about Power BI. The audience uses it. Some of them built reports in it.
  The point isn't "we replaced it" — the point is "we found a gap it wasn't designed to fill."

  The innovation here isn't the technology. It's the question we asked:
  "What does the team need to see TODAY to make a better decision TODAY?"

  Lean principle worth dropping here (lightly, don't lecture):
  Taiichi Ohno's idea of standing in a circle — go observe actual work before
  you try to improve it. These dashboards are our way of observing work digitally,
  in real time, without standing over anyone's shoulder.

  Transition: "So what did we build? Let me show you."

================================================================================
SLIDE 3 — "WHAT WE MEASURE AND WHY IT MATTERS"
Time: 2 minutes  (shown briefly before demo — leave it up during demo)
================================================================================

HEADLINE:
  "We measure process and capability — not people's worth."

VISUAL SUGGESTION:
  Three simple icons or columns. Clean, minimal.

  COLUMN 1: FLOW              COLUMN 2: QUALITY           COLUMN 3: SIGNAL
  "Is work moving?"           "Where does it break?"      "What's worth acting on?"
  WIP, Cycle Time,            Rework Ratio,               SPC Control Limits,
  Bottlenecks, Blocked        Hidden Factory,             Leading Indicators,
  Stories                     Bug patterns                Trend lines

BULLET POINTS TO HIT:
  • Six dashboards — Dev, BA, QA, UX, Business Process, Leadership
  • Each one asks: what does THIS role need to see to do better work?
  • Continuous improvement only works when you can see where you are
  • These are not scorecards. They are mirrors.
  • A good process produces good outcomes. We measure the process.

KEY TALKING POINT — SAY THIS OUT LOUD:
  "Accountability can feel uncomfortable when you're not used to it.
   But when you have data that helps you advocate for yourself —
   data that shows your team is understaffed, or blocked, or taking on
   too much WIP — that same data becomes a security blanket.
   It stops being used against you and starts working for you."

SPEAKER NOTES:
  This is the most important slide philosophically. Don't rush it.
  The audience will have a quiet anxiety about being measured — address it directly.

  The Lean framing: we are measuring capability and process health.
  A control chart with a point outside the limits isn't a failing grade —
  it's a question worth asking. "Something changed — do we know what it was?"

  Reference the book lightly: we've been developing a Lean and Agile framework
  internally that ties these measurements back to established principles —
  things like Little's Law, Theory of Constraints, value vs. waste.
  The dashboards are the practical application of that thinking.

  EOS connection (if it feels right to mention): this is how we build
  data into our operating model. Not as an add-on — as a foundation.
  Traction starts with visibility.

  Transition: "Let me show you what this looks like in practice."

DEMO SECTION — "LET ME SHOW YOU"
Time: 12 minutes total
================================================================================

--- DEMO BEAT 1: Dev Dashboard — Team Flow (3 min) ---

WHAT TO OPEN: Dev Dashboard → Team Flow lens

POINTS TO HIT WHILE NAVIGATING:
  • "This is real data — our team's actual ADO work items, live"
  • Point to WIP total: "Little's Law says WIP × Cycle Time = Throughput.
    When WIP climbs, cycle time climbs with it. The work slows down."
  • Point to a bottleneck signal (blocked items, aging work):
    "This is our digital Andon cord. In a Toyota plant, anyone can pull
    a cord to stop the line when something's wrong. This is the software equivalent."
  • Point to team breakdown: "Each team sees their own slice.
    Not compared to each other — compared to their own history."
  • "No names on this view. We're looking at flow, not individuals."

--- DEMO BEAT 2: Leadership Dashboard — Activity Table (2 min) ---

WHAT TO OPEN: Leadership Dashboard → Developer Activity section

POINTS TO HIT:
  • "This is where names appear — but look at what we're measuring"
  • Cards, PRs, commits, reviews, active days — inputs to the process
  • "Activity is not productivity. A developer who reviewed 20 PRs this
    sprint created enormous value that doesn't show up in story points."
  • Point to LD-12 Leading vs. Lagging section:
    "This is one of the most important ideas in measurement.
    Lagging indicators tell you the score after the game.
    Leading indicators help you win while you're still playing."

--- DEMO BEAT 3: SPC Control Charts — LD-13 Cycle Time (4 min) ---

WHAT TO OPEN: Leadership Dashboard → Cycle Time Control Chart (LD-13)

THIS IS YOUR SHOWSTOPPER. SLOW DOWN HERE.

POINTS TO HIT:
  • "This is Statistical Process Control — a concept from manufacturing
    quality engineering, applied to software delivery."
  • Point to the mean line: "This is our average cycle time — how long
    it takes a story to go from activated to closed."
  • Point to UCL/LCL: "These are control limits — not targets, not goals.
    They represent the natural variation of our process."
  • Point to a red outlier (if one exists):
    "A point outside these limits isn't a failure. It's a signal.
    Something was different about that item. Maybe it was a dependency.
    Maybe it sat in review too long. The chart doesn't tell us why —
    it tells us WHERE to ask the question."
  • "Walter Shewhart invented this in 1924 at Bell Labs.
    W. Edwards Deming brought it to Japan. We're using it on our sprints."
  • Key line: "Most performance problems are process problems, not people problems.
    Deming said 94% of failures belong to the system. This chart helps us see that."

--- DEMO BEAT 4: Rework Ratio + Burnout Signal (3 min) ---

WHAT TO OPEN: LD-15 Rework Ratio, then LD-16 Burnout Risk

REWORK (1.5 min):
  • "This is the hidden factory — a Lean concept for work that happens
    but doesn't show up in official metrics."
  • "Every rework item is a story about a process that could be stronger —
    a requirement that wasn't clear, a handoff that didn't land, a test
    that didn't catch something. No blame. Just signal."

BURNOUT (1.5 min):
  • "This one I want to be careful about — and that carefulness is intentional."
  • "This uses a statistical measure called a z-score to flag when someone's
    activity is significantly higher than their teammates' over time."
  • "This is NOT a productivity ranking. A high z-score might mean someone
    is energized and deep in a project. Or it might mean they're covering
    for understaffing. The chart prompts a CONVERSATION — it doesn't
    answer the question."
  • "This is the security blanket I mentioned earlier. If you're carrying
    more than your share, this makes it visible. Data you can take to
    your manager. Data that advocates for you."

SLIDE 4 — "WHERE WE GO FROM HERE"
Time: 2 minutes
================================================================================

HEADLINE:
  "This is a beginning, not a destination."

VISUAL SUGGESTION:
  Simple PDCA wheel or a clean roadmap with 3 phases.
  OR: just three clean bullet groups.

BULLET POINTS TO HIT:

  RIGHT NOW:
  • Six dashboards live and in use
  • SPC control charts in the Leadership view — proving the concept
  • Lean and Agile measurement framework in development (the book)

  NEXT:
  • React/modern rewrite — same concepts, better experience
  • Connect measurements to sprint ceremonies — use the data in the room
  • Expand SPC to more metrics as we validate the approach

  THE BIGGER IDEA:
  • This is an operating model, not a reporting tool
  • EOS thinking: visibility → accountability → traction
  • Continuous improvement isn't a project. It's a practice.

CLOSING LINE (say this, don't put it on the slide):
  "We didn't build these dashboards to watch people.
   We built them so the team could watch the process —
   and own what they find.
   The goal isn't perfect metrics. The goal is a team that
   gets a little better every sprint, knows why, and can prove it."

SPEAKER NOTES:
  Keep this short. The demo did the work.
  End on culture, not technology.
  Leave the audience with the feeling that this is something they could
  participate in — not something being done to them.

  If time allows, mention the book project briefly:
  "We're also documenting the thinking behind this — a Lean and Agile
  framework written for software teams, grounded in what we're actually
  measuring. We'll be sharing that as it develops."
